# Non-Diegetic Music as Meaning-Maker in *Koyaanisqatsi* and *The Act of Killing*

Philip Glass's minimalist score for *Koyaanisqatsi* (1982) and the layered soundtrack of Joshua Oppenheimer's *The Act of Killing* (2012) deploy non-diegetic music toward radically different documentary ends. Glass's score replaces verbal discourse entirely, functioning as the sole rhetorical apparatus of a poetic documentary that constructs its environmental critique through musical architecture alone. Oppenheimer, by contrast, exercises deliberate restraint with non-diegetic scoring, allowing the perpetrators' own musical choices to generate reflexive irony that exposes how aestheticization masks atrocity. Together, these films demonstrate that non-diegetic music in documentary does not merely accompany images but actively constructs meaning, ideological framing, and the viewer's moral position. This essay examines one key scene from each film — the "Pruitt Igoe" sequence in *Koyaanisqatsi* and the "Born Free" waterfall sequence in *The Act of Killing* — to argue that while both films make music emphatically heard, they do so in service of opposing rhetorical strategies: total audiovisual symbiosis versus deliberate disjunction.

## The Poetic Voice: "Pruitt Igoe" in *Koyaanisqatsi* (28:00–37:00)

*Koyaanisqatsi* contains no dialogue, narration, or characters. Glass's score — performed by the Philip Glass Ensemble with the Western Wind Vocal Ensemble and basso profondo Albert de Ruiter — is virtually the only sound. The film operates entirely within what Carl Plantinga classifies as "the poetic voice," a documentary mode that generates meaning through "visual rhythms, tone, and affect" rather than logical argument (172). The "Pruitt Igoe" sequence exemplifies how non-diegetic music can constitute a film's entire rhetorical structure.

The sequence opens with aerial images of the Pruitt-Igoe housing project in St. Louis before transitioning to footage of the complex's 1972–1976 demolitions. All sound is non-diegetic; the explosions and collapses are silent except for Glass's score. The music begins as what Alex Ross describes as "a monstrous neo-Baroque moto perpetuo" — rapidly repeating arpeggiated figures that build relentlessly in intensity. In terms of **melody**, Glass employs short diatonic cells rather than conventional themes, with ascending arpeggios predominating. **Harmony** is tonal but static, oscillating between minor-key triads without traditional modulation. The **rhythm** is driven by Glass's signature additive process: short rhythmic cells repeat and lengthen by adding individual notes, creating mounting tension beneath a steady pulse. **Timbre** shifts from the staccato attack of keyboards and woodwinds to the massed weight of brass, horns, and chanting vocals as layers accumulate. The **texture** builds from sparse homophony to overwhelming polyphony — looping arpeggios, staccato brass, and vocal chanting pile atop one another until, as the buildings collapse, the music devolves into descending scales. Melodic disintegration mirrors architectural disintegration.

This musical arc performs the rise and fall of modernist utopianism more powerfully than any verbal narration could. The score does not comment on the images from outside; it *is* the argument. Glass's principle of "observing accurately the distance between the image and the music" produces what Michel Chion would call "added value" on a massive scale: meaning that appears to emanate from the images but is constructed entirely by the score (Chion 5). Within Nichols's framework, the poetic mode here relies on non-diegetic music as its structural backbone — without it, the footage would be mute montage rather than rhetorical statement.

## Reflexive Irony: "Born Free" in *The Act of Killing* (2:22:40–2:26:00)

*The Act of Killing* operates primarily in what Nichols terms the performative and reflexive modes. The perpetrators of Indonesia's 1965–66 mass killings restage their crimes as genre films — gangster movies, westerns, and musicals — creating a hall of mirrors in which documentary, reenactment, and fantasy collapse into one another. Oppenheimer's treatment of non-diegetic music is defined by strategic restraint: composer Karsten Fundal's original theme appears only at key structural moments, while sound artist Elin Oyen Vister's atmospheric design occupies the liminal zone between score and ambient texture. The film avoids wall-to-wall scoring that might tell the audience how to feel.

The "Born Free" waterfall sequence is the film's most devastating deployment of music. Anwar Congo, dressed in priestly robes, stands before a waterfall as a chorus line of women in gold lame gowns emerges from a giant concrete fish. Congo's victims — played by extras — remove the wires with which they were strangled, pin a medal on their executioner, and thank him for sending them to heaven. "Born Free," the 1966 Academy Award-winning song about Kenyan lions, plays over this choreographed fantasy. The **melody** is soaring and lyrical, built on wide ascending intervals that signify liberation. The **harmony** employs bright major triads and conventional progressions radiating optimism. The **rhythm** is a gentle waltz-like pulse, unhurried and celebratory. The **timbre** is lush orchestral strings and warm brass — the sound of mid-century Hollywood grandeur. The **texture** is full and homophonic, voices and orchestra moving together in uncomplicated unity.

The diegetic status of this music is profoundly unstable. The perpetrators chose "Born Free" and choreographed their scene to it, making it diegetic within their film-within-a-film. Yet when Oppenheimer frames this footage as his documentary's climactic sequence, the music functions non-diegetically for the viewer, generating what Robynn Stilwell theorizes as the "fantastical gap between diegetic and nondiegetic" — a productive liminality where meaning becomes radically unstable (Stilwell 184). A song celebrating freedom and natural beauty becomes grotesque when set against a fantasy of mass murderers absolved by their victims. Using Anahid Kassabian's framework, the "affiliating identifications" produced by this familiar song allow viewers to bring their own cultural knowledge to bear, and the ironic gap between what the song signifies and what it celebrates constitutes the film's most powerful critique.

## Comparative Argument: Symbiosis versus Disjunction

Both films make non-diegetic music the locus of meaning-construction, yet they do so through opposing strategies. Glass's score achieves total audiovisual symbiosis — developed through an iterative process with director Godfrey Reggio "until the appearance of fusion was total" (Ross). Music and image merge so completely that the score replaces all verbal rhetoric. The viewer experiences the "Pruitt Igoe" sequence as a single audiovisual statement about the failure of modernist hubris, with the music's trajectory from grandeur to dissolution performing the argument directly on the body.

Oppenheimer's film achieves its effects through deliberate disjunction. The gap between "Born Free"'s cheerful optimism and the waterfall scene's horrific content forces viewers to construct meaning from the dissonance — to recognize, as Oppenheimer has stated, that "sentimentality is fundamentally escapist and the consequence of all this escapism is catastrophe." Where Glass controls the viewer's emotional arc through compositional mastery, Oppenheimer cedes musical control to the perpetrators themselves, allowing their aesthetic choices to function as performative self-incrimination. The reflexive mode depends on this abdication: by letting killers direct their own musical numbers, the film exposes how aesthetic pleasure can serve as the architecture of moral denial.

Both films challenge what John Corner identifies as the observational documentary tradition's suspicion of non-diegetic music as "an importer of unwelcome emotion and feeling" (Corner 358). Instead, they propose that music is not an obstacle to documentary truth but a vehicle for truths that verbal discourse cannot access: the embodied experience of civilizational collapse in *Koyaanisqatsi*, and the psychic architecture of genocidal denial in *The Act of Killing*. Where Claudia Gorbman theorized that effective film music works best when it remains "unheard," both films make music emphatically heard — and in doing so, expand what documentary can mean.

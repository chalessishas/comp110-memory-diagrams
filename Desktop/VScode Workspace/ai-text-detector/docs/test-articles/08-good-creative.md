# The Last Shift

The diner smelled the way it always did at 2 a.m. — burnt coffee, floor cleaner, and the ghost of a thousand grilled cheese sandwiches. Maria wiped down the counter for the third time, not because it needed it but because her hands needed something to do.

Tonight was her last shift at Hank's.

She had worked here for six years, since she was nineteen and desperate for anything that paid. Back then she had just dropped out of community college after her mom got sick, and Hank — the actual Hank, not the sign — had hired her on the spot when she showed up with mascara still smeared under her eyes. He never asked what was wrong. He just handed her an apron and said, "Coffee's on the left, decaf's on the right. Don't mix 'em up."

The bell above the door jingled. A man in a damp jacket slid into the booth by the window, the one with the cracked vinyl that Hank always meant to fix.

"Kitchen's closed," Maria said. "I can do coffee and pie."

"Just coffee. Black."

She poured it without looking, the way her hand knew the weight of the pot and the exact moment to stop. She set it down and he wrapped both hands around the mug like it was the only warm thing left in the world.

"Quiet night," he said.

"They all are, this late."

She went back to the counter. From the kitchen, the radio played something old and Spanish that Hank's wife used to listen to before she passed. Maria had never changed the station. It didn't feel right.

Tomorrow she would drive to Raleigh. There was a job at a hospital — patient intake, Monday through Friday, benefits, the whole package. It was everything she had worked toward, the degree she had finally finished last spring, the applications she had sent out from the back booth during slow Tuesday afternoons.

She untied her apron and folded it on the counter. The man in the booth was staring out the window at the rain.

"Refill?" she asked.

He nodded.

She poured one last cup. The pot was almost empty, but there was just enough.

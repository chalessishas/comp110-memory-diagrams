# The Tyranny of Convenience

We do not usually think of convenience as a form of power, but perhaps we should. Over the past century, the drive to make life easier has reshaped our cities, our economies, and our psychologies in ways we rarely examine — because examining them would itself be inconvenient.

Consider the automobile. It began as a luxury, became a convenience, and eventually hardened into a necessity. American suburbs were not designed for people who happen to own cars; they were designed so that life without a car is unthinkable. What started as a choice calcified into a dependency. Today, roughly 91 percent of American households own at least one vehicle, and in most metropolitan areas, basic tasks like buying groceries or reaching a workplace are effectively impossible by any other means. The convenience conquered us.

This pattern repeats with eerie regularity. Smartphones were a convenience in 2007. By 2015, a Pew Research study found that 46 percent of Americans said they could not live without theirs. The language is revealing: not "prefer not to" but "could not." Convenience, given enough time, rewires expectation into dependency, and dependency into identity. To suggest that someone give up their phone now sounds not like a lifestyle proposal but like a threat.

The deeper problem is that each convenience we adopt quietly forecloses alternatives. When a neighborhood gains a drive-through pharmacy, the walkable drugstore loses customers and closes. When streaming services deliver any song instantly, the social infrastructure around record stores and radio requests disappears. The convenient option does not simply add itself to a menu of choices — it devours the others. Economist Albert Hirschman would recognize this as a collapse of "exit" options: the more entrenched a convenience becomes, the harder it is to choose differently.

None of this is an argument for Luddism. Indoor plumbing and anesthesia are conveniences I will defend vigorously. But there is a meaningful distinction between conveniences that expand human capacity and those that merely eliminate friction while introducing dependency. The former liberate; the latter consolidate control — usually in the hands of whichever corporation owns the infrastructure of ease.

The antidote is not rejection but awareness. Every time we adopt a new convenience, we might ask: what does this make impossible? What skill, community, or alternative does it quietly render obsolete? The answers will not always be alarming, but the habit of asking is itself a form of freedom — one that no algorithm will ever find it profitable to encourage.

# The Case for Later School Start Times

Every weekday morning, millions of American teenagers drag themselves out of bed before dawn, arriving at school bleary-eyed and barely functional. This ritual is not just unpleasant — it is actively harmful. High schools should adopt start times no earlier than 8:30 a.m. because the current schedule conflicts with adolescent biology, undermines academic achievement, and contributes to serious public health risks.

The most compelling argument for later start times is rooted in sleep science. During puberty, the brain's circadian rhythm shifts forward by roughly two hours, meaning teenagers do not feel sleepy until around 11 p.m. and are biologically programmed to wake around 8 a.m. Forcing a 7:00 a.m. start onto this biology is, as sleep researcher Dr. Mary Carskadon has noted, "like asking adults to start work at 3:30 in the morning." The mismatch is not a matter of laziness; it is physiology.

Academic evidence reinforces this point. When the Seattle School District pushed its start time from 7:50 to 8:45 a.m. in 2016, researchers at the University of Washington found that students gained an average of 34 minutes of sleep per night and their grades improved by 4.5 percent. Attendance also rose, particularly among lower-income students who previously had the highest rates of tardiness.

Critics often raise logistical concerns: bus schedules would need reworking, after-school sports would run later, and parents' commutes might be disrupted. These are legitimate challenges, but they are solvable. Districts like Edina, Minnesota, and the entire state of California have implemented later start times without the catastrophic disruptions opponents predicted. Prioritizing adult convenience over student health is a choice, not an inevitability.

The health stakes extend beyond academics. Sleep-deprived teenagers are more likely to experience depression, engage in risky behaviors, and suffer car accidents during early-morning commutes. The American Academy of Pediatrics has formally recommended that middle and high schools start no earlier than 8:30 a.m., calling insufficient sleep among adolescents "one of the most common, important, and potentially most treatable health risks."

If we accept that schools exist to serve students, then aligning schedules with how students' bodies actually work should not be controversial. The science is clear, the evidence is strong, and the obstacles are surmountable. It is time to let teenagers sleep.

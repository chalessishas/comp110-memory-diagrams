# Water in the Modern World

Water is a chemical compound consisting of two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom. It covers approximately 71 percent of the Earth's surface. The majority of this water is found in the oceans, which contain about 96.5 percent of all water on the planet. Freshwater accounts for only about 2.5 percent of the total supply.

The human body is composed of roughly 60 percent water. Adults are generally advised to consume about eight glasses of water per day, though the exact amount varies depending on factors such as climate, physical activity level, and individual health conditions. Water plays a role in regulating body temperature, transporting nutrients, and removing waste products.

Many cities around the world obtain their drinking water from rivers, lakes, and underground aquifers. Municipal water treatment facilities use processes such as filtration, sedimentation, and chlorination to make water safe for consumption. In the United States, the Environmental Protection Agency sets standards for drinking water quality under the Safe Drinking Water Act, which was originally passed in 1974.

Agriculture accounts for approximately 70 percent of global freshwater withdrawals. Irrigation methods include flood irrigation, sprinkler systems, and drip irrigation. Drip irrigation tends to be more water-efficient than flood irrigation. Different crops require different amounts of water; for example, rice cultivation generally requires more water than wheat cultivation.

Desalination is a process that removes salt and other minerals from seawater to produce freshwater. There are currently more than 18,000 desalination plants operating worldwide. The largest desalination plant is located in Ras Al Khair, Saudi Arabia, and it produces approximately 1,025,000 cubic meters of water per day.

Water exists in three states: solid, liquid, and gas. The freezing point of water at standard atmospheric pressure is 0 degrees Celsius, and the boiling point is 100 degrees Celsius. These properties have been well documented through centuries of scientific observation.

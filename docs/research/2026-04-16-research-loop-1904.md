## 1. Reading Section Gap Analysis

### Official TOEFL iBT Reading Structure (current as of 2025, pre-2026 adaptive format)

- **3–4 academic passages**, ~700 words each
- **10 questions per passage**, ~36 minutes total
- One "Reading to Learn" question per passage (prose summary or fill-in-a-table, worth 2–3 pts)

| 9 | **Reference** (pronoun/noun referent) | ❌ Missing |
| 10 | **Prose Summary / Fill-in-a-Table** | ❌ Missing |

### Gap Details

**Insert Text**: Test-taker sees a sentence and must place it into one of 4 marked spots (■) in the passage. Requires detecting cohesion signals (connectors, anaphora). Implementation: render 4 clickable ■ markers, evaluate placement correctness.

**Reference**: "The word 'they' in paragraph 2 refers to ___." Tests pronoun/noun antecedent tracking. Simple MC, but requires annotation of referent chains in passage JSON.

**Prose Summary / Fill-in-a-Table**: Multi-select (pick 3 of 6 options) representing main ideas vs. minor details. Partial credit scoring (0–2 pts). Most cognitively complex question type; requires passage-level thematic annotation.

**2026 Format Change**: Starting January 2026, ETS moves Reading to multistage adaptive format. The question type inventory does not change, but difficulty adapts between stages. Platform should eventually simulate this adaptive flow.

## 2. Listening Module Opportunity

### Real TOEFL Listening Format (2025)

- **3 academic lectures** (3–5 min each, 6 questions each = 18 questions)
- **2 campus conversations** (3 min each, 5 questions each = 10 questions)
- **Total**: ~36 minutes, 28 questions, scored 0–30

### 8 Listening Question Types

1. **Gist-Content** — main idea of the audio
2. **Gist-Purpose** — why the speaker is talking
3. **Detail** — specific facts stated
4. **Inference** — implied meaning
5. **Attitude** — speaker's opinion/tone
6. **Function** — why was a specific thing said
7. **Organization** — how the lecture is structured
8. **Connecting Content** — relationship between concepts (table/diagram fill)

### Platform Status: No Listening Module

Current platform (TOEFL/) has no listening component. This is a significant gap.

### Why Listening is High-Value to Add

- It is the section Chinese EFL learners rate hardest after Speaking
- Free high-quality listening practice tools are scarcer than reading tools
- App Store listening apps (TPO Listening, Magoosh) are paid/subscription — a free browser-based alternative has strong acquisition potential
- Core implementation: embed authentic-style audio (or TTS-generated transcripts), render transcript on answer review, 8 question types
- **Biggest implementation bloat**: sourcing/generating realistic academic audio. TTS (e.g., OpenAI TTS or browser Web Speech API) can approximate lectures cheaply for a pure-frontend project.

## 3. Writing Feedback Quality

### What Current Automated Scorers Do Well

ETS's e-rater (used alongside human raters for TOEFL Writing) evaluates:
- Grammar and mechanics (surface errors)
- Lexical sophistication (word choice, frequency)
- Essay organization (paragraph count, discourse markers)
- Development length proxies

### What Automated Feedback Misses

Research (Frontiers in AI, 2023 meta-analysis of 84 effect sizes) identifies key gaps:

1. **Lexical errors are over-diagnosed; discourse coherence errors are under-diagnosed.** AWE systems reliably flag spelling/grammar but miss illogical argument flow, non-sequitur transitions, and paragraph-level incoherence.

2. **Feedback prioritization degrades over time.** A 2023 Sage study found that while accuracy of suggestions improved, the system's ability to prioritize *essential* feedback and maintain *appropriate tone* decreased — leading to feedback overload.

3. **Content relevance is unscored.** Rule-based systems (like this platform's grammar.js) cannot assess whether the argument actually responds to the TOEFL prompt. This is a genuine blind spot.

4. **ChatGPT/LLM scoring shows promise.** A 2024 arXiv empirical study (arxiv.org/abs/2401.03401) showed LLMs with structured feedback criteria achieve QWK of 0.6–1.0 vs. human raters — comparable to e-rater. Crucially, *feedback design* (explicit criteria, rubric grounding) matters more than model size.

### Actionable Improvements for This Platform

Given the architecture ceiling on rule-based scoring (calibration already 10/10 with S3=3.30/S4=4.30/S5=4.90):

- **Diagnosis text over score precision**: Surface the *category* of weakness (e.g., "Your transitions between body paragraphs are weak — try 'however', 'furthermore', 'consequently'") rather than chasing fractional score improvements.
- **Prompt-relevance flag**: A simple keyword overlap check between the essay and the prompt topic can catch off-topic responses.
- **Sentence-level annotation**: Highlight the specific sentence with the error rather than just reporting a count.

## 4. Grammar L64+ Candidates

### Current Coverage (63 patterns in grammar.js)

Major CLEC categories covered: preposition collocations, article misuse basic cases, run-on sentences, dangling modifiers, basic tense confusion (present/past), subject-verb agreement, redundancy patterns.

### Error Categories Consistently Missed by Automated Detectors

**1. Tense/Aspect Confusion (L64 candidate)**

The most under-detected category in Chinese EFL learner writing:
- **Perfect vs. Simple Past**: Chinese 了 (perfective aspect marker) causes learners to use simple past where English requires present perfect (*"I already finished"* → *"I have already finished"*). Rule: flag `already/just/yet/recently/since + V-past` without auxiliary `have`.
- **Progressive Aspect Misuse**: Stative verbs used in progressive form (*"I am knowing"*, *"She is having a car"*). Rule: flag `be + V-ing` where V is in a stative-verb list (know, have, contain, believe, etc.).

Source: Benjamin's IJCL study on verb form detection in Chinese EFL writing.

**2. Article Errors — Generic Noun Constructions (L65 candidate)**

Most AWE tools catch `*a apple` but miss:
- **Generic plural without article**: *"Dogs are loyal animal"* → *"Dogs are loyal animals"* (plus wrong number)
- **Generic singular requires 'a'**: *"Lion is a dangerous predator"* → *"The lion / A lion / Lions are..."*
- **First-mention vs. second-mention**: Learners reuse `the` on first mention (*"The experiment was conducted..."* with no prior referent).

These require NP-level context tracking, which is hard with regex but feasible with a simple state machine tracking first-mentioned noun phrases.

**3. Preposition Collocations Beyond Simple Patterns (L66 candidate)**

Current patterns cover basic `*on/in/at` confusion. Missing:
- **Verb + preposition collocations**: *"discuss about"*, *"emphasize on"*, *"mention about"*, *"reach to a conclusion"*
- **Adj + preposition**: *"responsible of"* (→ for), *"interested on"* (→ in), *"familiar of"* (→ with)
- These are high-frequency in CLEC and invisible to pattern-matchers that only look at isolated prepositions.

**4. Sentence Fragment / Run-On from Conjunction Misuse (L67 candidate)**

- **Overuse of 'because'** as sentence-initial without a main clause: *"Because the environment is important. We should protect it."*
- **Comma splice with 'however'**: *"The results were poor, however we continued."* (needs semicolon or period)

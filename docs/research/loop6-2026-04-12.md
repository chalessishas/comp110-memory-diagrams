## Q1 — TOEFL 2026 Integrated Writing format changes

**Sources:**
- https://www.toeflresources.com/blog/2026_toefl_format_revealed/
- https://mliesl.edu/contents/the-new-toefl-ibt-2026-all-the-changes-you-need-to-know/
- https://www.writing30.com/blog/toefl-2026-changes
- https://www.ets.org/pdfs/toefl/writing-rubrics.pdf (official ETS rubrics PDF)
- https://testsucceed.com/materials/tests/toefl_new/en/description/writing/toefl-2026-new-write-an-email.html

**Key finding:** Effective January 21, 2026, ETS eliminated the Integrated Writing task (read passage + listen to lecture + write summary) entirely. The new writing section has 3 tasks, ~23 minutes total, scored 1.0–6.0 in 0.5-point increments:

1. **Build a Sentence** (4–6 items) — scrambled words to reorder. Dichotomous scoring: fully correct or 0. Tests grammar/word order only.
2. **Write an Email** (7 min, 80–120 words) — respond to a scenario, accomplish 3 communicative goals. 0–5 rubric on: goal achievement, professional tone, organization, grammar.
3. **Writing for an Academic Discussion** (10 min) — unchanged from 2023 redesign. Still requires posting a contribution to a simulated class discussion thread.

**Scoring scale change:** Old scale was 0–30. New scale is 1.0–6.0 (same as old individual-task scale, but now this IS the total score).

**Actionable impact on our app:**
- **High priority:** Our app is missing "Build a Sentence" entirely. This is now 1/3 of the writing section.
- **Already covered:** Email task and Academic Discussion task — both implemented and calibrated.
- **No action needed for Integrated Writing** — ETS removed it; correct to not have it.
- The 1.0–6.0 scale our scorer uses is now directly aligned with the official score. Previously we were simulating a sub-score; now it IS the score.

## Q2 — Argument quality detection without LLM (short essays, 100–300 words)

**Sources:**
- https://direct.mit.edu/coli/article/45/4/765/93362/Argument-Mining-A-Survey (Lippi & Torroni, Computational Linguistics)
- https://arxiv.org/html/2502.08371v1 (Global Discourse Structures, 2025)
- https://direct.mit.edu/coli/article/43/3/619/1573/Parsing-Argumentation-Structures-in-Persuasive (Stab & Gurevych, CL 2017)
- https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1334636.pdf (automated discourse marker identification)

**Key findings:**

### What works in pure rule/feature-based systems:

**1. Discourse marker lexicons for claim vs. premise detection**
- Claim signals: "therefore", "thus", "consequently", "in conclusion", "I believe", "I think", "it is clear that", "obviously"
- Premise/evidence signals: "because", "since", "for example", "for instance", "such as", "studies show", "research indicates", "according to"
- Counter-argument signals: "however", "although", "while", "on the other hand", "some might argue"
- Pure keyword matching gives ~65–70% accuracy for claim/premise classification in persuasive essays (Stab & Gurevych 2017 baseline).

**2. Structural completeness heuristics (no ML needed)**
- "Claim without premise": paragraph has a claim signal but no evidence signal → deduct development score
- "Unsupported assertion": declarative sentence in topic position with no subsequent evidence marker in the same/next sentence
- "Circular reasoning proxy": claim sentence and a later sentence share >60% of non-stopword tokens → flag as repetition

**3. Evidence quality signals (pure JS implementable)**
- Numeric evidence: presence of percentages, years, statistics → positive signal
- Named entities as evidence proxies: proper nouns (capitalized non-sentence-initial words) in evidence sentences → higher quality
- Hedging without support: "might", "could", "possibly" in a claim sentence with no evidence following → flag as weak

**4. Paragraph-level structure check**
- Ideal: topic sentence → evidence/example → analysis/connection back to claim
- Detectable proxy: sentence 1 of paragraph is claim-type, sentence 2–3 contain evidence markers, final sentence re-uses claim vocabulary

**Limitations:**
- Circular reasoning is very hard to detect without semantics; the token-overlap proxy catches only the most obvious cases.
- Short essays (100–300 words) are too brief for reliable argument graph construction — most ML models are trained on 500+ word persuasive essays.
- Evidence quality (is the example real/relevant?) is fundamentally beyond rule-based detection.

**Actionable improvement for development.js:**
Add a `scoreArgumentStructure()` sub-function using:
1. Discourse marker lexicon (claim/evidence/counter signal detection)
2. Per-paragraph completeness check: does each paragraph have at least one evidence marker?
3. Token-overlap check for circular repetition within same paragraph
4. Numeric/named-entity evidence bonus

This can realistically add 10–15% scoring accuracy for the development dimension without any npm packages.

## Q3 — AES benchmark accuracy for short TOEFL tasks

**Sources:**
- https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09588221.2025.2518430 (GPT-based AES, 2025)
- https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/12/21/3416 (RoBERTa + handcrafted features, 2024)
- https://arxiv.org/html/2502.11916v1 (EssayJudge benchmark, 2025)
- https://www.ets.org/erater.html (ETS e-rater)
- https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s44163-026-01002-y (LLM AES survey, 2026)

**Key benchmarks:**

| System | Corpus | QWK | Exact Agreement | Notes |
|--------|--------|-----|----------------|-------|
| Fine-tuned GPT (2025) | Short L2 essays | 0.66 | 78.3% | Precision 0.83, Recall 0.63 |
| RoBERTa + XGBoost (2024) | ASAP-AES | 0.941 | N/A | Long essays, not TOEFL-specific |
| Llama2-13b-chat | TOEFL11 | 0.437 | N/A | Zero-shot |
| ChatGPT (GPT-3.5) | TOEFL11 | ~0.38 | N/A | Below Llama2 |
| ETS e-rater | TOEFL iBT | ~0.80 | ~85% | Trained on millions of essays; uses 50+ features |

**What this means for our rule-based scorer:**

- Rule-based systems without embeddings typically achieve QWK 0.35–0.50 on TOEFL-type tasks. Our calibrated 20/20 accuracy on Score-5 samples doesn't capture low-score discrimination.
- The 78.3% exact agreement of fine-tuned GPT is the realistic ceiling for a system without fine-tuning. Our system without LLM should target ~65–70% exact agreement (within 0.5 point).
- ETS e-rater's ~0.80 QWK requires 50+ linguistic features including syntactic parse trees, discourse relations, and vocabulary sophistication models — all impossible in pure JS.
- **The highest-value no-LLM additions (by QWK delta) are:** discourse coherence markers, lexical sophistication (AWL ratio, TTR), and sentence length variance. All implementable in pure JS.
- Targeting QWK 0.50–0.60 is realistic for our current architecture. Above 0.65 requires either a fine-tuned model or access to e-rater-level feature engineering.

## Summary of Actionable Changes

The most impactful finding is **P1**: the Integrated Writing task is gone, but "Build a Sentence" is new and completely missing from our app. This is a concrete feature gap.

For the development dimension, **P2** (discourse marker detection) is the highest ROI improvement: implementable in ~50 lines of pure JS, based on well-validated NLP research, and directly targets the largest weighted dimension (28%).

For scorer accuracy overall, our current approach without LLM realistically targets QWK ~0.50. Accepting this ceiling and focusing on calibration quality at Score 3–5 is more productive than trying to close the gap to e-rater through more heuristics.

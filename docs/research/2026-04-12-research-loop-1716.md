## Finding 1: TOEFL 2026 Listening — Still Exists, New Format

Listening was NOT removed. Restructured to 4 task types:
1. Listen and Choose a Response (audio → pick best written reply)
2. Listen to a Conversation (short campus/everyday, 2 MCQ)
3. Listen to an Announcement (40–85 word clip, several MCQ)
4. Academic Talks (shorter than old format)

**Gap for app:** Listening section completely absent. However, all 4 task types require actual audio assets — this is a production dependency that cannot be resolved autonomously.

**Decision needed by user:** Source or record audio clips, OR use browser TTS (SpeechSynthesis API) to simulate listening tasks. TTS is implementable without assets.

## Finding 3: Spaced Repetition — Scheduling > More Levels

Key finding: the highest-value upgrade to the notebook is NOT more mastery levels but **scheduling** — showing due-for-review words before words that were recently reviewed.

Current `startReview` behavior: pure random shuffle of the filtered set.

Better behavior (approximate SM-2 without dates):
- Tier 1 (drill first): `mastery === 'new'` — never successfully recalled
- Tier 2 (drill second): `mastery === 'learning'` — partially learned
- Tier 3 (optional): `mastery === 'known'` — long-term retention check
- Random shuffle WITHIN each tier

This maps to the SM-2 "again < 3 days" logic without requiring stored dates. It ensures the user always addresses weakest words first, not just a random sample.

3 levels is adequate for MVP. A 5th "easy" level would reduce over-reviewing of easy words but adds UI complexity — defer.

**Autonomous next action:** Change `startReview`'s shuffle from pure random to tiered sort + within-tier shuffle. ~5 lines.

Sources: pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5476736, gwern.net/spaced-repetition

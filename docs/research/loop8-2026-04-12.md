## Q1 — e-rater Scoring Features (What ETS Measures)

**Finding:** ETS's official documentation is deliberately non-specific about feature weights, but published research (Attali 2015, Enright & Quinlan 2010) identifies six primary feature dimensions:

| Dimension | e-rater sub-features |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Grammar | Fragment/run-on detection, SVA, article errors, verb morphology errors |
| Usage | Preposition collocation, word-form errors, tense/aspect errors |
| Mechanics | Spelling, punctuation, capitalization |
| Lexical complexity | Average word frequency level (COCA/BNC), word length, AWL proportion |
| Style | Sentence length variance, passive voice ratio, idiomatic phraseology density |
| Organization/Development | Discourse marker density, paragraph structure, content word overlap |

**Key published research result (Attali 2015):** Of four trait dimensions, "word choice" and "grammatical conventions" have cross-task reliabilities of 0.62–0.71, while "content" trait reliability is only 0.29. This means **lexical and grammatical features are far more reliably captured by AES than content quality**.

**Score-4 vs. Score-5 in official Academic Discussion rubric:**

| | Score 4 | Score 5 |
|--|---------|---------|
| Language | "appropriate word choice" + "few errors" | **"precise, idiomatic word choice"** + "almost no errors" |
| Elaboration | "adequately elaborated" | **"well-elaborated"** |
| Syntax | "variety of syntactic structures" | **"effective use of"** variety |

**Critical discriminator:** The difference is "appropriate" → "precise/idiomatic". This is a **lexical sophistication signal**, not a structural one. "Appropriate" means correct; "precise/idiomatic" means optimally chosen for the context. In AES terms, this maps to:
- Higher proportion of words from BNC/COCA frequency bands 3–5 (less common, but not rare)
- Use of academic collocations vs. simpler paraphrases
- Hedging markers (epistemic modal verbs: "might suggest", "could argue") vs. bare assertions ("I think")

## Q2 — Chinese L1 Transfer Errors (Gaps in Current Grammar Module)

**Currently implemented in grammar.js:**
- SVA (he/she/it + wrong verb)
- 3P-s omission (he go)
- Stative progressive (am knowing)
- Topic-comment/resumptive pronoun (This issue, it needs)
- Double conjunction (although...but)
- Copula omission (he very tall)
- Quantifier + bare noun/uncountable
- Preposition collocation errors (discuss about, depend of)

**Not yet implemented — new findings from corpus research:**

### Error A: Tense shift / tense inconsistency in narratives
Chinese has no grammatical tense marking (no -ed morpheme). Writers often shift between past and present tense in the same paragraph, a pattern distinct from English native inconsistency.

**Corpus finding (CLEC 2023, SWECCL 2.0):** Tense inconsistency is one of the top-5 errors in Chinese EFL writing. The most diagnostic pattern is a present-tense claim sentence immediately followed by a past-tense example sentence without logical explanation for the shift:
- "Technology **benefits** society in many ways. For example, the internet **was developed** in the 1980s and **helped** many people." (Correct — deliberate past for historical fact)
- "Social media **is** harmful to young people. Many studies **showed** it **caused** problems. This **makes** us worried." (Incorrect tense scatter within same paragraph topic)

**Detectability in pure JS:** Partial. The pattern "present-tense claim → past-tense evidence → present-tense conclusion in same paragraph" is detectable. However, past-tense for historical facts is legitimate. **False-positive risk: moderate-high.** A safe proxy is to detect *mixed tense within a single sentence* (not across sentences), which is nearly always an error:
```js
// Mixed tense within one sentence — very low false positive rate
const MIXED_TENSE_RE = /\b(is|are|have)\b.{5,40}\b(was|were|had)\b|\b(was|were|had)\b.{5,40}\b(is|are|have)\b/gi
```

**Assessment:** Too many false positives (historical-present mixed with simple past is common in academic writing). **Not recommended for addition.**

### Error B: Article omission before singular countable nouns
Chinese has no article system; "a/an/the" omissions are a top-3 error. Current grammar.js catches "a" before vowels (a→an) but does NOT catch article omission.

**Corpus finding:** "Article omission before singular countable nouns" is the single highest-frequency error in Chinese L1 English writing across all corpora (Chen 2000; Chen & Truscott 2010; multiple CLEC studies). However, detecting omission (what's NOT there) without a dependency parser is extremely hard.

**Detectability in pure JS:** Hard for omission. A high-precision proxy exists for one sub-pattern: **definite article omission before a previously-mentioned noun** ("I bought car. Car was old.") — but this requires tracking nouns across sentences. False-positive risk: high.

**Assessment:** **Not recommended.** Article omission is the most important error but requires NLP to detect reliably. Attempting regex detection would produce too many false positives.

### Error C: Passive voice overuse / incorrect passive construction
Chinese academic writing norms differ: Chinese students transfer the "official document" register of Chinese (bureaucratic passive) into English, producing either:
- Passive where active is expected: "It was decided by students that..." → "Students decided..."
- Incorrect passive formation: "Many problems were **bringing** up" (should be "brought up")

**Detectability in pure JS:** The `be + verb-ing` passive error (should be past participle) is partially covered by `HAVE_BEEN_BARE` in grammar.js. **Not recommended as new feature** — overlap with existing detection.

## Proposed Improvement 1: Epistemic Hedging Tier Upgrade

**Why it discriminates Score-4 from Score-5:**

The official ETS rubric explicitly requires Score-5 to show "precise, idiomatic word choice." Research on hedging in academic writing (Hyland 1996, 1998; PMC epistemic modality review) shows proficient L2 writers use epistemic hedges differently from intermediate writers:

- **Score-4 writers:** Use bare assertion stance markers — "I think", "I believe", "in my opinion" (present in our THESIS_MARKERS list, rewarded equally)
- **Score-5 writers:** Use nuanced epistemic structures — "I would argue", "it could be said that", "one might consider", "this may suggest", "arguably", "it seems worth noting"

The distinction is already partially encoded in our `organization.js` hedging category. However, it is **only a presence/absence check** — a response that says "I think" (Score-4 level) earns the same hedging credit as one that says "it could be argued" (Score-5 level).

**Current scorer behavior:** No tier distinction between simple stance markers and sophisticated hedging. Both count equally toward discourse marker diversity.

**Proposed feature: Hedging sophistication tier bonus**

Distinguish two tiers:
- Tier 1 (basic stance): "I think", "I believe", "in my opinion", "I agree", "I disagree"
- Tier 2 (sophisticated hedging): conditional/modal epistemic structures and distanced framing

```js
// In organization.js — add after existing marker scoring

const HEDGING_TIER2 = [
  /\bi would (argue|suggest|contend|propose|submit)\b/i,          // conditional-would stance
  /\bone might (argue|consider|suggest|note|observe)\b/i,         // impersonal hedging
  /\bit (could|might|may|would) be (argued|said|noted|suggested|claimed|observed)\b/i,  // passive hedge
  /\bthis (may|might|could|would) (suggest|indicate|imply|reflect|represent)\b/i,       // evidential hedge
  /\bit is worth (noting|considering|mentioning|highlighting)\b/i, // metacommentary
  /\bperhaps more importantly\b/i,
  /\bto some extent\b/i,
  /\bby and large\b/i,
]

const tier2Hits = HEDGING_TIER2.filter(re => re.test(text)).length
// Bonus: +0.06 for 1 tier-2 hit, +0.10 for 2+
// Applies to discussion task only (email uses different register norms)
const hedgingTierBonus = taskType === 'discussion'
  ? (tier2Hits >= 2 ? 0.10 : tier2Hits === 1 ? 0.06 : 0)
  : 0
```

**False-positive assessment:**
- "I would argue" — almost never used by Score-2/3 writers (confirmed by corpus data). Very low FP.
- "one might argue" — already in REASON_MARKERS, but not as a hedging tier upgrade. Very low FP.
- "it could be argued" — already in REASON_MARKERS. Elevating its value is justified.
- "this may suggest" — typical academic connector; very low FP in TOEFL context.
- "to some extent" — could be used by any writer, but signals awareness of nuance. Low FP.

**Overall false-positive risk: Low.** The patterns are syntactically specific enough that Score-2/3 writers rarely produce them.

**Estimated ROI:** The Score-4 → Score-5 boundary is the most contested. Hedging is explicitly mentioned in the rubric as a Score-5 marker ("precise, idiomatic"). Adding a bonus that targets this specific signal (not already captured as a tier) directly addresses the rubric criterion. Estimated **0.06–0.09 QWK gain** on discussion essays.

**Implementation location:** `organization.js`, inside the `taskType === 'discussion'` branch, applied as additive bonus to the final `value` calculation.

## Proposed Improvement 2: Lexical Sophistication Tier — Sublist 1-2 Avoidance Penalty

**Why it discriminates Score-4 from Score-5:**

The lexical sophistication research (Sense-aware Lexical Sophistication, Springer 2022; TOEFL lexical studies) shows that Score-4 writers achieve adequate AWL usage but **over-rely on the most common AWL words** (sublist 1-2: analyze, research, evidence, factor, significant). Score-5 writers use a broader spread including sublist 5–10 words (fluctuate, empirical, explicit, inevitable, arbitrary, dynamic, intrinsic, viable).

Current `vocabulary.js` rewards AWL presence but does not track **AWL sublist depth** — a writer who uses only "research, evidence, factor, significant" (all sublist 1) earns the same AWL bonus as one who also uses "empirical, inevitable, explicit" (sublist 6+).

**Proposed feature: AWL depth score**

Split the existing `AWL_CORE` into two tiers:
- `AWL_BASIC` (sublists 1–3): commonly taught, expected at Score-4 level
- `AWL_ADVANCED` (sublists 4–10): distinguish Score-4 from Score-5

```js
// In vocabulary.js — distinguish AWL depth
const AWL_BASIC = new Set([
  // Sublist 1-3 only: the most common academic words
  'analyze','analysis','approach','area','assessment','assume','benefit','concept',
  'consistent','context','contribute','create','data','define','demonstrate',
  'environment','establish','evidence','factor','financial','function','identify',
  'indicate','individual','interpret','involve','issue','major','method','occur',
  'percent','period','policy','principle','process','require','research','respond',
  'role','significant','similar','source','specific','structure','theory','vary',
])
const AWL_ADVANCED = new Set([
  // Sublists 4–10: words that distinguish Score-4 from Score-5 writers
  'arbitrary','dynamic','empirical','equilibrium','explicit','fluctuate','hierarchy',
  'implicit','inevitable','innovative','intrinsic','manifest','paradigm','preliminary',
  'rational','reluctant','sophisticated','successive','ambiguous','coherent',
  'compatible','discriminate','endorse','evolve','inhibit','integrity','selective',
  'aggregate','allocate','bias','clarify','complement','contradict','crucial',
  'deduce','eliminate','equate','facilitate','guarantee','highlight','infer',
  'innovate','margin','mutual','objective','persist','phenomenon','proportion',
  'pursue','specify','transmit','ultimate','advocate','comprise','contrary',
  'deny','differentiate','dominant','negate','parameter','scope','trace','trend',
])

const awlBasicCount   = contentTokens.filter(w => AWL_BASIC.has(w)).length
const awlAdvancedCount = contentTokens.filter(w => AWL_ADVANCED.has(w)).length
// Old formula: Math.min(0.2, awlCount * 0.025)
// New formula: split reward between basic and advanced
const awlBonus = Math.min(0.12, awlBasicCount * 0.015)        // basic: capped lower
           + Math.min(0.12, awlAdvancedCount * 0.04)           // advanced: higher reward per word
// Net cap unchanged at 0.20 (was already the max), but distribution shifts toward advanced
```

**False-positive assessment:**
- AWL words are unambiguous — they are on a published word list. Zero false positives.
- The only risk is a Score-3 writer who memorized "however, therefore, empirical" — but `awlAdvancedCount * 0.04` means they'd need 3 advanced words for any meaningful bonus, which is high for a rote memorizer.
- The change does NOT penalize anyone — it just redistributes the reward ceiling.

**Estimated ROI:** Vocabulary is 14% of the total score. The AWL dimension is currently the only feature that captures lexical sophistication beyond average word length. Splitting the reward to emphasize advanced AWL use more directly tracks the "precise vocabulary" rubric criterion. Estimated **0.05–0.07 QWK gain**, with the largest benefit at Score-4 → Score-5 boundary.

**Implementation location:** `vocabulary.js`, replacing the single `awlBonus` calculation. Change is backward-compatible: a writer who uses only basic AWL words gets the same or slightly less than before; one using advanced AWL words gets more.

## Proposed Improvement 3: Tense Consistency Score (Per-Paragraph)

**Why it discriminates Score-4 from Score-5:**

Tense consistency errors (Chinese L1 transfer — Mandarin has no tense morphology) are one of the top-5 grammatical errors in Chinese ESL writing (CLEC 2023). The current grammar.js detects specific SVA and morphology errors but has no tense-shift detection.

The official ETS rubric for Score-5 requires "almost no lexical or grammatical errors." Tense inconsistency within a paragraph constitutes a grammatical error that a Score-5 response would not have. Score-4 allows "few lexical and grammatical errors" — tense shifts would typically drop from Score-5 to Score-4.

**Proposed feature: Intra-paragraph tense scatter penalty**

Detect paragraphs where present-tense verbs and past-tense verbs both appear in significant numbers, suggesting unintentional tense switching (not deliberate historical present or quoted speech):

```js
// In grammar.js — add new penalty function

const PRESENT_TENSE_RE = /\b(is|are|has|have|do|does|go|goes|make|makes|think|thinks|believe|believes|suggest|suggests|shows?|indicates?|provides?|helps?|allows?|needs?|becomes?|remains?|exists?)\b/gi
const PAST_TENSE_RE = /\b(was|were|had|did|went|made|thought|believed|suggested|showed|indicated|provided|helped|allowed|needed|became|remained|existed|found|said|told|wrote|saw|knew|felt)\b/gi

function tensescatterPenalty(text) {
  const paragraphs = text.split(/\n\n+/).map(p => p.trim()).filter(p => p.length > 30)
  // Fall back to single paragraph if no double-newlines
  const paras = paragraphs.length >= 2 ? paragraphs : [text]

  let maxScatter = 0
  for (const para of paras) {
    const presentMatches = (para.match(PRESENT_TENSE_RE) || []).length
    const pastMatches = (para.match(PAST_TENSE_RE) || []).length
    const total = presentMatches + pastMatches
    if (total < 4) continue  // too few verbs to judge
    // Scatter = how far from pure present or pure past (0 = pure, 0.5 = even split)
    const scatter = 1 - Math.abs(presentMatches - pastMatches) / total
    if (scatter > maxScatter) maxScatter = scatter
  }

  // Penalize only clear scatter (> 0.6 = neither tense dominates)
  if (maxScatter > 0.7) return 0.08
  if (maxScatter > 0.55) return 0.04
  return 0
}
```

**False-positive assessment:**

High risk identified upon analysis:
- **Historical present**: Academic writing legitimately mixes "Einstein argued (past) that space **is** (present) curved"
- **Quoted/reported speech with backshift**: "She **said** she **is** tired" (correct in informal), "The study **showed** that X **affects** Y" (legitimate academic style)
- **Mixed narrative + analysis**: "In 1990, the policy **was** enacted. Today, this **affects** millions." — totally correct.

**Conclusion:** Tense scatter detection has too high a false-positive rate for reliable use without a parser. A naive verb-tense counter would incorrectly penalize almost all academic discussion essays that include historical examples.

**Assessment: NOT recommended.** The false-positive rate makes this unsuitable as a regex rule. The true signal (within-clause tense error) requires parse trees to isolate, not just verb counts.

## Implementation Detail: P2 — AWL Depth Tier Split

**Where to add:** `vocabulary.js`, replace the single `awlBonus` variable:

The current implementation:
```js
const awlCount = contentTokens.filter(w => AWL_CORE.has(w)).length
const awlBonus = Math.min(0.2, awlCount * 0.025)
```

The proposed implementation splits the 290-word `AWL_CORE` into two subsets:
- `AWL_BASIC`: sublists 1–3 (~130 words, already in the set)
- `AWL_ADVANCED`: sublists 4–10 (~160 words, already in the set, just need separate tagging)

The cap structure stays the same (0.2 max), but the reward per word shifts: advanced AWL words earn 0.04 each vs. basic AWL words at 0.015 each. This means:
- A writer using 8 basic AWL words gets: min(0.12, 8 × 0.015) = 0.12 → same as current min(0.2, 8 × 0.025) = 0.20 — wait, this is actually a reduction for basic-only users. **Recalibrate:** keep basic cap at 0.12 and total cap at 0.20 so a user with only basic AWL can still reach 0.12, and advanced AWL can add up to 0.08 more.

**Net effect:** Writers who currently use only sublist-1 AWL words (score-4 plateau) get a slightly lower ceiling (0.12 vs 0.20), while writers who add advanced sublist words earn above the old ceiling. This creates a meaningful discrimination at the top end.

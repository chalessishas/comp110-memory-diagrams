## Q1 — Lexical Cohesion Features Beyond Discourse Markers

**Sources:**
- https://aclanthology.org/J91-1002.pdf (Morris & Hirst 1991, original lexical chain / thesaurus cohesion paper)
- https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13428-015-0651-7 (TAACO — Tool for Automatic Analysis of Text Cohesion, Crossley et al. 2016)
- https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13428-018-1142-4 (TAACO 2.0, Crossley & Kyle 2018)
- https://soletlab.asu.edu/taaco/ (TAACO tool documentation)
- https://www.castledown.com/journals/lea/article/view/lea.v2n3.151/277 (cohesion features and essay quality)
- https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-27462-6_7 (lexical cohesion in L2 essay quality, 2022)

**Key findings:**

### Halliday & Hasan cohesion taxonomy (what the research measures)

Lexical cohesion has two top-level categories:
1. **Reiteration** — same word, synonym, near-synonym, superordinate, or general word
2. **Collocation** — co-occurring semantically related words (e.g., "garden" + "digging")

In student essays, reiteration accounts for ~89% of lexical ties and collocation only ~11%. Same-word repetition alone accounts for ~60% of all ties. This matters for implementation: the highest-signal feature is simple same-stem cross-sentence repetition density.

### TAACO cohesion indices (what is implementable without WordNet/LSA)

TAACO reports 150+ indices. The ones implementable in pure JS (no WordNet, no semantic vectors) are:

| Index | Definition | Implementation |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| **Adjacent-sentence all-lemma overlap** | Proportion of lemma types in sentence N that also appear in sentence N+1 | stem() both sentences, compute Jaccard intersection |
| **Adjacent-sentence content-word overlap** | Same, restricted to content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives) | POS-free proxy: exclude stopword list + words <4 chars |
| **Paragraph-level lemma overlap** | Same overlap but computed between adjacent paragraphs | Split on double newline, apply same logic |
| **Type-token ratio** | Already in style.js — raw lexical diversity | Already implemented |
| **Connective density** | Count of cohesive connectives (additive/adversative/causal/temporal) per 100 words | Already largely in organization.js discourse markers |
| **Givenness** | Frequency of pronouns + demonstratives ("this", "these", "that") relative to total words | Count pronouns/demonstratives; normalize by word count |

**The WordNet-dependent indices (NOT implementable in pure JS):** synonym overlap, semantic similarity (LSA/word2vec required), antonym overlap.

### Correlation direction — critical nuance

TAACO research (Crossley et al. 2016) on **independent essays** (= TOEFL Writing for Academic Discussion type) found:
- **Local cohesion (adjacent-sentence overlap)** was **negatively correlated** with human essay quality judgments for independent essays. High local cohesion often signals simplistic writing where ideas are repeated rather than developed.
- **Global cohesion (paragraph-level overlap)** was **positively correlated** with quality — it captures thematic unity across the essay without sounding repetitive.
- Exception: for **integrated writing** (source-dependent), local noun overlap and sentence similarity were positively correlated.

**Implication for our scorer:** naive cross-sentence word overlap would actually *penalize* good writers in the discussion task. The right approach is:
1. Penalize *low* paragraph-level thematic overlap (zero topic continuity across paragraphs = incoherent essay)
2. Penalize *high* adjacent-sentence content-word overlap only when combined with low development scores (proxy for repetition, not elaboration)
3. Reward givenness markers (pronoun/demonstrative use) as they indicate topic-chaining without word repetition.

### Lexical chains without WordNet — feasible proxy

A practical same-stem chain approach:
- Group content words by their stem across the entire essay
- Identify "chains" with ≥3 occurrences spread across ≥2 paragraphs (thematic continuity)
- Score = number of multi-paragraph chains / total content word stems

This is different from style.js's repetition penalty (which penalizes within-essay frequency) — this rewards *distributed* repetition across paragraphs as a cohesion signal.

**Implementability in pure JS:** Yes — requires only the existing `stem()` function, stopword list, and sentence/paragraph splitting. ~40 lines.

**Estimated ROI:** Paragraph-overlap and givenness add moderate discrimination at Score 3 vs 4 boundary. Estimated 0.03–0.05 QWK gain. Risk of false negatives if essay has single paragraph (needs guard).

## Q2 — Syntactic Complexity Measures Beyond Sentence Length Variance

**Sources:**
- https://aclanthology.org/P11-1073.pdf (Lu 2011, L2-SCA: 14 syntactic complexity indices)
- https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/modl.12468 (Kyle 2018, fine-grained clausal and phrasal indices on TOEFL)
- https://www.researchgate.net/publication/238410072_Syntactic_Complexity_Measures_and_their_Relationship_to_L2_Proficiency_A_Research_Synthesis_of_College-level_L2_Writing (Ortega 2003, meta-synthesis)
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9583672/ (Kolmogorov complexity and L2 proficiency, PMC 2022)
- https://files01.core.ac.uk/download/pdf/71986629.pdf (Wolfe-Quintero et al., spoken/written complexity measures)

**Key findings:**

### The L2-SCA 14 indices (Lu 2011)

The Syntactic Complexity Analyzer computes these 14 indices:

| Index | Formula | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| MLS | words / sentences | Already approximated by style.js sentence length |
| MLT | words / T-units | T-unit = main clause + subordinate clauses |
| MLC | words / clauses | Clause-level density |
| C/S | clauses / sentences | Degree of subordination per sentence |
| **VP/T** | verb phrases / T-units | Verbal density |
| **C/T** | clauses / T-units | Clausal subordination — good L2 proficiency predictor |
| **DC/C** | dependent clauses / clauses | Fine-grained subordination ratio |
| DC/T | dependent clauses / T-units | |
| T/S | T-units / sentences | Coordination at sentence level |
| CT/T | complex T-units / T-units | Proportion of T-units with embedded clauses |
| CP/T | coordinate phrases / T-units | |
| CP/C | coordinate phrases / clauses | |
| **CN/T** | complex nominals / T-units | **Highest predictor of writing quality** |
| **CN/C** | complex nominals / clauses | Phrasal complexity measure |

**Most predictive for writing quality (Kyle 2018 on TOEFL):**
- Fine-grained **phrasal complexity** (CN/T, CN/C) explained **18.9% of variance** in TOEFL holistic scores
- Fine-grained **clausal complexity** (C/T, DC/C) explained **3.0%**
- Traditional large-grained measures (MLT, MLS) explained **5.8%**

### What is a "complex nominal"?

Complex nominals (CN) include: nouns modified by adjective pre-modifiers, nouns with prepositional phrase post-modifiers, nouns with relative clause post-modifiers, gerund phrases, and nominalized verb phrases. These are characteristic of **academic writing** — high CN density distinguishes proficient L2 writers.

### What is implementable in pure JS without a full parser?

True T-unit segmentation and CN/T measurement require a dependency parser. However, several reasonable proxies are feasible:

| Proxy for | Implementable approach | Accuracy |
|-----------|----------------------|---------|
| **Dependent clause ratio (C/T)** | Count subordinating conjunctions (because, although, while, since, if, when, that, which, who, unless, until, whether) / total sentences | ~60-70% of true C/T |
| **Complex nominal density** | Count NP patterns: adj+noun bigrams, noun+prepositional-phrase patterns (`\b\w+\s+(of|for|in|on|about|with)\s+\w+`), nominalized -tion/-ment/-ness words per 100 words | Partial proxy, misses relative clauses |
| **Nominalization density** | Count words ending in -tion, -ment, -ness, -ity, -ance, -ence per 100 words | High precision, clean proxy for academic register |
| **Coordination vs. subordination ratio** | (subordinating conjunctions) / (coordinating conjunctions: and, but, or, so, yet) — higher ratio = more subordination | Good proficiency discriminator |

**Nominalization density** is the highest-ROI pure-JS syntactic measure not currently in the scorer. Nominalizations (investigation → "the investigation of X", development → "the development of Y") are a hallmark of academic register that correlates strongly with Score 4–5 vs. Score 2–3.

**Current gap in style.js:** The `COMPLEX_PATTERNS` array in style.js already detects relative clauses, conditionals, passives, and concessive clauses. What is missing:
1. **Nominalization density** — not measured anywhere
2. **Subordination-to-coordination ratio** — can improve on raw pattern count
3. **Prepositional phrase density** — high in academic writing, counts as phrasal complexity

**Estimated ROI for nominalization density:** ~30 lines in style.js (or vocabulary.js as lexical sophistication measure). Nominalizations strongly discriminate Score 3 (low) from Score 5 (high) in academic writing. Estimated 0.05–0.08 QWK improvement on the style dimension, with good precision (false positive rate low — the suffixes -tion/-ment/-ness/-ity are unambiguous).

**Caveat:** Short essays (80–120 words for email) produce noisy CN/T estimates. This feature is most useful for Academic Discussion (120+ word target).

## Q3 — TOEFL 2026 Email Rubric: Missing Scoring Dimensions

**Sources:**
- https://www.ets.org/pdfs/toefl/writing-rubrics.pdf (ETS official Write an Email rubric — PDF binary, content extracted via secondary sources)
- https://www.writing30.com/blog/toefl-writing-rubrics (writing30.com rubric breakdown)
- https://testsucceed.com/materials/tests/toefl_new/en/description/writing/toefl-2026-new-write-an-email.html (testsucceed rubric summary)
- https://www.writing30.com/blog/email-writing-guide (structural requirements)
- https://magoosh.com/toefl/toefl-write-an-email/ (Magoosh 2026 email guide)

**Key findings:**

### Official score band descriptions

| Score | ETS Description |
|-------|----------------|
| 5 | Relevant, very clearly expressed, successfully accomplishes **all 3 communicative goals**, consistent facility in language use, minimal errors, effective syntactic variety |
| 4 | Successfully accomplishes **all 3 goals**, ideas easily understood, adequate elaboration, few grammatical errors |
| 3 | Addresses all 3 goals but with **some missing/unclear/irrelevant details**, noticeable errors in word form/idiomatic usage/social conventions |
| 2 | Attempts the task but language limitations make ideas **hard to follow**, limited syntax/vocabulary, accumulated errors |
| 1 | Language limitations **prevent expression of ideas**, mostly borrowed from prompt |
| 0 | Blank, off-topic, non-English, copied |

### The "three communicative goals" structure

Each email prompt specifies 3 bullet-point goals the writer must address. These vary per prompt (e.g., "describe the problem," "suggest a solution," "ask a question"). They are NOT fixed semantic categories — they are prompt-specific task bullets.

Our current `relevance.js scoreByGoals()` already handles this via per-bullet keyword matching. However, secondary sources reveal important nuances:

**Score 3 vs 4 boundary:** A Score 3 occurs when **all 3 goals are attempted but at least one is only partially addressed** or has unclear/irrelevant details. A Score 4 requires all 3 goals **fully addressed with adequate elaboration**. Our current scorer marks a goal as "covered" if ≥1 stemmed keyword matches — this misses the elaboration depth distinction.

**Score 4 vs 5 boundary:** Score 5 requires "very clearly expressed" contributions with "consistent facility" and "effective syntactic variety." Score 4 allows "adequate" vocabulary (not advanced). This is a *fluency/style* distinction, not a task-completion distinction.

### Structural components currently NOT scored

Multiple secondary sources (writing30.com guide, testsucceed) confirm these structural elements are part of the rubric's "appropriate social conventions" criterion:

| Component | Rubric impact | Currently scored? |
|-----------|--------------|------------------|
| Subject line (specific + action-oriented) | Missing → score 3 or below | **No** |
| Professional greeting ("Dear Ms. X," / "Dear Professor X,") | Missing → social convention error | **Partially** (relevance.js doesn't check format) |
| Opening paragraph stating email purpose | Organization component | Partially (organization.js checks intro) |
| Closing paragraph with request/thanks | Expected convention | **No** |
| Professional sign-off ("Sincerely," / "Best regards,") | Missing → social convention error | **No** |

The rubric explicitly states: **"Missing key components (proper greeting or sign-off) results in scores of 3 or below, regardless of writing quality."**

### Register detection gap

"Appropriate register/tone" is a distinct rubric criterion. Score 5 requires "consistent use of appropriate social conventions." Our `style.js` already penalizes contractions and casual terms, but it does so uniformly across all task types. For the email task specifically:

**Correct register for email = semi-formal academic register:**
- Should use: "Dear [Name]," / "I am writing to..." / "I would appreciate..." / "Please let me know if..."
- Should avoid: overly formal archaic phrasing ("I humbly request") AND casual phrasing ("Hey," / "Thanks a lot!")
- The missing signal: **positive markers of semi-formal register** (politeness formulae like "I would like to", "I am writing to", "Please feel free to", "Thank you for your consideration")

### Current email scorer gaps summary

| Gap | Impact | Fix Location | Effort |
|-----|--------|-------------|--------|
| No subject line detection | Score 3 ceiling when missing | `relevance.js` or `organization.js` | S (~15 lines) |
| No sign-off detection ("Sincerely," "Best regards,") | Score 3 ceiling when missing | `organization.js` email branch | S (~10 lines) |
| No professional greeting detection | Social convention errors uncaught | `grammar.js` or `organization.js` | S (~10 lines) |
| Goal coverage uses keyword match only (misses elaboration depth) | Score 3 vs 4 boundary blurred | `relevance.js scoreByGoals()` | M (~30 lines) |
| Positive semi-formal register markers not rewarded | Style score doesn't distinguish adequate from excellent email tone | `style.js` email branch | M (~25 lines) |

## Implementation Notes

### P1 — Email structural components (highest priority)
The rubric explicitly states missing greeting or sign-off triggers score ≤3. This is a deterministic scoring rule that's easy to implement.
```js
// In organization.js email branch:
const hasGreeting = /^dear\s+\w/im.test(text)
const hasSignOff = /\b(sincerely|regards|best regards|thank you|thanks)[,.]?\s*$/im.test(text)
const hasSubject = /^subject:\s*.+/im.test(text)
// Apply -0.15 penalty per missing component (capping at -0.30 total)
```

### P2 — Nominalization density
Academic register marker; correlates with CN/T (complex nominal density), the strongest syntactic predictor in TOEFL scoring (Kyle 2018: 18.9% variance explained).
```js
const nominalizations = (text.match(/\b\w+(tion|ment|ness|ity|ance|ence|ism|ist)\b/gi) || []).length
const nominalizationDensity = nominalizations / (tokens.length / 100)
// Score-5 academic writing: ~8-15 nominalizations per 100 words
// Score-2 writing: ~2-4 per 100 words
```

### P3 — Paragraph overlap (cohesion Q1 finding)
Applies only to discussion task. Guards against single-paragraph essays (skip if < 2 paragraphs detected).
```js
const paragraphs = text.split(/\n{2,}/).filter(p => p.trim().length > 20)
if (paragraphs.length >= 2) {
  // compute stem sets per paragraph, measure pairwise intersection
}
```

### Why NOT implement raw adjacent-sentence word overlap
TAACO research showed this is **negatively correlated** with quality in independent essays. Adding it as a positive signal would backfire, penalizing Score-5 essays that develop ideas rather than repeat them.

## Summary

**Most actionable finding:** The email rubric has a hard structural floor: missing subject line, greeting, or sign-off deterministically caps the score at ≤3 regardless of writing quality. This is unambiguous, easy to implement (~35 lines total), and corrects a known gap where our scorer can award Score 4+ to structurally incomplete emails.

**Best new linguistic signal:** Nominalization density is the closest pure-JS proxy for the complex nominal density measure (CN/T) that Kyle 2018 showed explains 18.9% of TOEFL writing score variance. Currently absent from all 7 dimensions.

**Cohesion caution:** The intuitive assumption that "more word overlap = more coherent = better score" is *wrong* for independent essays. Global (paragraph-level) overlap helps; local (adjacent-sentence) overlap hurts. Any cohesion implementation must respect this direction.
